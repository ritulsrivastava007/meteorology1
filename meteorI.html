Global & Ancient Roots in Meteorology

Meteorology's history spans ancient civilizations: Aristotle's 'Meteorologica' (4th century BCE) classified phenomena; Indian sages in Vedas and Varahamihira predicted weather. Today, international bodies continue this legacy.

The Who's Who in Global Meteorology

World Meteorological Organization (WMO)

Building on ancient Greek (Aristotle) and Chinese observations, WMO established in 1950 coordinates global weather/climate/water efforts, sets standards, issues warnings, supports climate research via 193 members.

Key functions include the World Weather Watch program, which provides real-time data exchange, and leadership in the Global Climate Observing System (GCOS).

Visit WMO

India Meteorological Department (IMD)

Ancient roots in India trace back to Vedic texts (Rigveda ~1500 BCE) describing monsoons and clouds, and Brihat Samhita by Varahamihira (6th century CE) with rainfall predictions using planetary positions. Modern IMD founded in 1875 operates vast observatories for forecasts, cyclone warnings, agrometeorology.

IMD manages monsoon forecasting, earthquake monitoring (National Centre for Seismology), aviation services (ICAO compliant), continuing ancient traditions with modern science for disaster preparedness.

Visit IMD

International Air Federation (IAF)

The FAI (Fédération Aéronautique Internationale), founded in 1905, governs world records in aeronautics and promotes safe air sports practices worldwide. It integrates meteorological data for event planning and safety.

In meteorology, FAI works with ICAO and national authorities to provide airspace information and weather-aware guidelines for gliding, parachuting, and ballooning competitions.

Visit IAF

General Circulation of the Atmosphere

The global atmospheric circulation, often termed the "General Circulation of the Atmosphere" (GCA), is the large-scale movement of air masses driven by differential solar heating at the equator versus poles. This uneven heating generates convection currents forming three primary cells per hemisphere: the tropical Hadley cell (driving trade winds), mid-latitude Ferrel cell (westerlies), and polar cell.

These cells create pressure belts—equatorial low, subtropical highs, subpolar lows, polar highs—resulting in prevailing winds that distribute heat, moisture, and influence monsoons, jet streams, and storm tracks globally.
