Global Meteorological Data Exchange

The backbone of modern meteorology is a sophisticated network of data exchange protocols and telecommunication systems. The World Meteorological Organization's Global Telecommunication System (GTS) connects over 3000 stations worldwide, transmitting terabytes of weather data daily. Satellite imagery from INSAT, GOES, and Meteosat series, combined with ground-based radars and automatic weather stations (AWS), form a continuous stream feeding numerical weather prediction (NWP) models.

In India, the India Meteorological Department (IMD) operates a dedicated fiber-optic backbone linking 500+ observatories with redundancy for cyclone warnings. Data formats like BUFR (Binary Universal Form for the Representation of meteorological data) ensure interoperability across nations.

Solar to Sidereal Time Converter

Sidereal time lags solar time by approximately 3 minutes 56 seconds per day due to Earth's orbital motion. Astronomers and satellite operators use this correction for precise orbital predictions and stellar observations.

Input solar time (HH:MM:SS UTC) to compute corresponding sidereal time, essential for G-rib somesonde launches and radio occultation missions.

METAR Aviation Terminal

METAR (Meteorological Aerodrome Report) is aviation's universal weather code, packing wind, visibility, clouds, temperature, pressure into 30-50 characters. Issued hourly (or half-hourly at busy airports), decoded instantly by pilots and ATC systems. Example: VIDP 251230Z 25015G25KT 8000 FEW030 BKN100 32/22 Q1008 – Delhi, variable winds gusting 25kt, 8km vis, scattered clouds, 32°C.

IMD generates METARs for 150+ airports, integrated with ICAO standards. Special reports (SPECI) trigger on visibility <10km, wind change >10kt, thunderstorms. During fog season, Delhi's IGI reports trigger runway closures saving millions.
